## Voice Pillars

The portfolio's tone rests on three words: **Approachable. Creative. Experienced.** Each word has a specific meaning in this context. They are not adjectives to describe Harsha — they are behavioral instructions for how the copy should perform.

---

### 1. Approachable

**What it means in practice:**
Copy talks to the reader like a smart colleague, not a consultant billing by the hour. Sentences are short. Language is plain. The reader should never need to re-read a sentence. No jargon, no hedging, no throat-clearing preambles. When Harsha explains the AI workflow, it sounds like an explanation — not a product pitch.

Approachable does not mean casual. It means accessible. A first-time founder and a VP of Product should both feel like this site was written for them specifically.

**What it sounds like:**
"I've worked across SaaS, fintech, healthtech, and enterprise software — with early-stage founders who needed to move fast, and product org leads who needed to move carefully."

**The opposite to avoid:**
Cold, formal, or corporate. Any sentence that could have been written by a consulting firm's marketing team. Copy that distances the reader from the person behind it. Also avoid being so casual it undermines credibility — no slang, no exclamation points, no "Hey!" openers.

---

### 2. Creative

**What it means in practice:**
The copy itself should demonstrate creative thinking — not just claim it. This means surprising specificity instead of generic claims, interesting structural choices (a contrast, an unexpected framing, a concrete detail where the reader expects abstraction), and descriptions that show the work rather than name it.

Creative does not mean clever for its own sake. The portfolio is a sales tool. Every creative choice must serve clarity or credibility — never distract from it.

**What it sounds like:**
"Research synthesis in hours, not weeks. More design directions per dollar. Documentation that engineers actually read."

**The opposite to avoid:**
Generic, predictable copy that sounds like it could appear on any designer's portfolio. Superlatives with no substance ("world-class," "exceptional"). Descriptions of the process without a concrete image of the outcome.

---

### 3. Experienced

**What it means in practice:**
Every claim is earned, not asserted. Experienced sounds like someone who has seen the same failure mode many times and learned to prevent it. It references real constraints, real friction, real client problems — not abstract design principles. When metrics appear, they are specific and contextualized. When outcomes are described, they describe what changed for the client's business — not what was delivered.

Experienced also means knowing what to leave out. The copy is confident enough to be concise. It does not oversell, over-explain, or pad claims with qualifiers.

**What it sounds like:**
"The product team was shipping inconsistent UI across 3 platforms. Accessibility regressions were caught late in QA, and designers spent 30% of their time on component audits rather than solving user problems."

**The opposite to avoid:**
Vague authority ("years of experience helping businesses grow"). Self-congratulation without evidence. Padding metrics with modifiers that undermine them ("up to 40% faster," "in most cases"). Any claim that cannot be grounded in a specific project or outcome.

## Audience-Specific Tone Calibration

The portfolio serves three client types. The tier labels (Startup / Growth / Enterprise) are internal navigation tools — not how clients describe themselves. Copy should lead with the problem the client is living, not the stage label Harsha assigns them.

---

### Startup

**Language that resonates:**
Direct. Energetic. Practical. Startup founders are time-poor and skeptical — they have heard every "I'll change your product" pitch. What works is specificity about the output and honesty about the timeline. They respond to evidence of speed and credibility, not process explanations.

**What they care about most:**
- Moving fast without making expensive design mistakes
- Looking credible to investors before the product is fully built
- Getting dev-ready output — not concepts that can't be built

**Their vocabulary:**
Ship, MVP, prototype, investor demo, user-validated, dev-ready, runway, traction, Series A, pitch, build

**How to frame the AI angle for this audience:**
Lead with outcomes. Do not mention the AI stack by name. The message is: you get more design thinking per dollar, faster than if you hired in-house. The AI is why — but the client doesn't need to know the mechanism to benefit from the result.

Approved framing: "Investor-ready design in weeks, not months. You get the depth of a senior in-house designer without the cost of hiring one."

NOT: "I use Claude and GPT-4 to accelerate the research phase."

**Sample approved headline for this tier:**
"Investor-ready design without the full-team price tag."

**Sample approved CTA:**
"Get your MVP designed"

---

### Growth

**Language that resonates:**
Precise. Systems-oriented. Growth-stage product leads and engineering managers talk in terms of velocity, consistency, and scale. They have heard "we'll fix the design debt" before — and it didn't happen. What works is showing that Harsha understands infrastructure, not just interfaces.

**What they care about most:**
- Design becoming a bottleneck that slows engineering
- Inconsistency across platforms that creates rework
- Handoffs that actually work in practice, not just in theory

**Their vocabulary:**
Design system, tokens, component library, handoff, consistency, scale, platform, sprint, velocity, multi-platform, ship, CI/CD integration, documentation

**How to frame the AI angle for this audience:**
Frame AI as a process accelerator that creates better infrastructure faster. They are technical enough to be interested in how it works, but skeptical enough to need proof it produces better output — not just faster output. Lead with the outcome, then optionally explain the mechanism.

Approved framing: "Design systems built 40% faster — with better documentation than most teams produce manually."

**Sample approved headline for this tier:**
"Design infrastructure that keeps up with your engineering team."

**Sample approved CTA:**
"Scale your product design"

---

### Enterprise

**Language that resonates:**
Measured. Governance-aware. Compliance-fluent. Enterprise stakeholders (Directors of UX, VPs of Product, programme owners) deal with complexity that founders do not — procurement, accessibility mandates, multiple internal stakeholders who each have veto power. They want to see that Harsha understands how things move slowly in large organisations — and has a plan for it.

**What they care about most:**
- Stakeholder alignment, not just design quality
- Compliance and governance (WCAG, accessibility, audit trails)
- Deliverables that hold up under scrutiny from executives and procurement

**Their vocabulary:**
Governance, compliance, WCAG, accessibility audit, stakeholder alignment, programme, phased rollout, executive presentation, design leadership, change management, enterprise-grade

**How to frame the AI angle for this audience:**
For this audience, the AI angle is most effective when framed around rigor and process transparency. Technical clients (including enterprise architects and senior engineers on these teams) will appreciate knowing the stack. Non-technical enterprise sponsors will appreciate knowing that accessibility checks are automated and documentation is thorough.

Two-track approach: For technical enterprise contacts, the AI workflow detail is a credibility signal. For non-technical sponsors, lead with governance and compliance outcomes — mention the AI approach only if they ask or if the RFP requires it.

Approved framing for technical contacts: "Accessibility validation is automated at every phase — not a final-stage audit."
Approved framing for non-technical sponsors: "Structured for governance, built for compliance — WCAG AA embedded from day one."

**Sample approved headline for this tier:**
"Design leadership that navigates stakeholder complexity."

**Sample approved CTA:**
"Discuss your programme"

## Anti-Vocabulary

Words and phrases that must never appear in copy for this portfolio. Each one weakens credibility, sounds generic, or contradicts the brand.

| Phrase | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| "Passionate about" | Every designer says this. It costs nothing and proves nothing. Replace with a specific claim about what the work produces. |
| "Love solving problems" | Generic to the point of meaninglessness. All designers claim this. Show a specific problem that was solved instead. |
| "Innovative" | Overused. Meaningless without context. If something is genuinely novel, describe what is new about it specifically. |
| "Leveraging AI" | Corporate jargon. "Using AI to..." is always clearer. If you use the word "leveraging" anywhere, delete it. |
| "Seamless experience" | A cliché so worn it signals a lack of original thinking. Replace with a specific description of what the experience does. |
| "World-class" | Unverifiable superlative. Never use without hard evidence, and even then it's usually unnecessary. |
| "End-to-end" | Overused in design portfolios. If the work covers the full lifecycle, describe the specific phases instead. |
| "Best-in-class" | Same problem as world-class. Sounds like marketing copy from a vendor's landing page. |
| "Cutting-edge" | Technical jargon that is immediately disbelieved. If something is current, name the specific technology. |
| "Thinking outside the box" | Disqualifying. Never use this phrase in any context. |
| "Deliverables that move the needle" | "Move the needle" is management consulting speak. Describe what specifically improved. |
| "Deep dive" | Overused in professional contexts. Replace with the specific activity: "audited," "researched," "tested." |
| "Take your product to the next level" | Vague aspiration with no substance. Every vendor claims this. |
| "I'm Harsha, a UX designer who..." | Weak bio opener. Lead with what you do for clients, not who you are. |
| "Passionate" (in any context applied to design work) | See "passionate about." Any use of this word in professional copy should be replaced with a claim about outcomes. |
| "Synergy" | No. |
| "Holistic" | Overused in design and health. Replace with specific description of scope. |
| "User-centric" | Every designer claims this. If the work puts users first, show how — through research methods, testing rounds, and outcomes. |
| "Bespoke" | British corporate jargon. Use "custom" if you mean custom, or be specific about what makes it unique. |
| "Tailored solutions" | Generic. The services section already demonstrates tailoring — this phrase is filler. |
| "Pixel-perfect" | Outdated and beside the point. The work is outcome-focused, not craft-focused. |
| "My passion for design..." | See "passionate." Delete. |

## Copy Standards

### CTA button format

Pattern: **Action verb + object** (optionally + qualifier)

- Primary CTAs: "See my work", "Start a conversation"
- Tier CTAs: "Get your MVP designed", "Scale your product design", "Discuss your programme"
- In-page CTAs: "Read the case study", "How I work"

Rules:
- Never use "Click here" or "Learn more" as standalone CTAs
- Never start a CTA with "I" (e.g. "I can help you" is a description, not a CTA)
- Secondary CTAs should be lower commitment than primary: "How I work" before "Start a conversation"
- CTAs in the services section should echo the client's problem language, not service language

---

### Case study headline format

Headlines should focus on the **outcome or transformation**, not the deliverable or process.

Pattern: **[What changed] for [who]** or **[Problem] → [Result]**

Approved examples:
- "A design system that engineers actually adopted" (not "Figma Component Library")
- "From 4 days to same-day: how a dashboard served two audiences at once" (not "AI Analytics Dashboard Redesign")
- "Field workers who stopped abandoning tasks mid-session" (not "Context-Aware Mobile App")

Rules:
- The headline should be writable from the outcomes section, not the deliverables section
- Avoid starting with the technology ("AI-powered...", "React-based...")
- Avoid category labels as headlines ("Enterprise SaaS," "Data Visualization") — these are tags, not headlines
- The current case study titles ("AI-Powered Design System," "AI Analytics Dashboard") are placeholder labels, not final headlines. When real projects are populated, rewrite using the outcome-first format.

---

### Bio copy rules

- Write in first person throughout
- Open with what you do for clients, not with "I'm Harsha, a designer who..."
- Do not use the word "passionate" — not once
- Reference specific industries or client types, not abstract claims about experience
- If a year or timeline is mentioned, it must be accurate
- Avoid the phrase "at the intersection of" — it is design-industry boilerplate
- The About section should feel like a person wrote it, not a LinkedIn profile generator
- Maximum one claim about AI per paragraph — do not front-load the bio with AI mentions; the AI workflow section handles that

Approved pattern for the opening paragraph:
"I'm a UX designer who [specific thing you do] — [specific outcome]. Over [N] years, I've worked with [client type 1] and [client type 2], specifically [what makes the work different]."

Current copy that passes this test:
"I'm a UX designer who's spent years building products at the intersection of user needs and business goals — and the last few years specifically building AI into every part of that process."

Note: "at the intersection of" is flagged as anti-vocabulary (design-industry boilerplate). When real bio copy is written, replace this phrase.

---

### Metric presentation

Metrics are the most credible copy on the page — and the easiest to undermine. Rules:

**Format rules:**
- Always pair a number with a specific context: "40% reduction in design-to-development handoff time" — not "40% faster delivery" (faster than what?)
- When two time periods are compared, show both: "3–5 days vs. 2–4 weeks" — not "4x faster"
- Round numbers (40%, 50%) need grounding context. Either name the project they came from, or qualify with "across [N] projects"
- Do not show percentages and multipliers for the same metric on the same page — pick the format that is more meaningful to the audience

**Credibility rules (critical — see harsha-answers.md):**
- The hero stats (40% faster, 20+ projects, 5.0 stars) are currently aspirational/placeholder. They must not appear as definitive claims until verified against real data.
- Until real stats are available, either: (a) remove the floating stat cards from the hero, or (b) reframe them as targets/benchmarks with honest framing ("What AI-accelerated design delivers: 40% faster turnaround on average")
- Testimonials are placeholder. Do not treat the attributed quotes as real client copy. Replace with real Upwork reviews as soon as available.
- Case study metrics (85% usability test success, 60% fewer support tickets, etc.) are also placeholder. When real projects are documented, use the actual measured outcomes.

**Recommended metric presentation format:**
> **[Number]** [specific metric]
> [context line: how it was measured, what project, or what the baseline was]

Example: "40% reduction in handoff time — measured across the design system project, compared to the previous sprint cycle."

### How to write about AI without sounding like a product demo

The AI workflow is a key differentiator — but the current site leans toward listing tools (Claude, GPT-4, Midjourney, Figma AI) in a way that reads like a vendor comparison page. The goal is to explain what AI makes possible for the client, not to demonstrate technical familiarity with AI tools.

**The core rule:** Name the outcome, not the tool. If the tool name must appear, it appears second — after the outcome it enabled.

Approved: "Research synthesis that used to take three weeks now takes three days — because pattern recognition across interview transcripts is something AI does well."

Not approved: "I use Claude and Dovetail AI to synthesize user research in the Discover phase."

**The four questions to answer when writing about AI:**
1. What phase of the design process does this apply to?
2. What was the client's experience before? (The problem)
3. What is different now? (The outcome)
4. What does the designer still do that AI cannot? (The human contribution — this is what earns trust)

Question 4 is the most important and the most frequently omitted. Every AI workflow description should make clear that Harsha is directing, evaluating, and making judgment calls — the AI is accelerating execution, not replacing design thinking.

**For technical audiences (enterprise engineers, technical leads):**
Naming the specific tools is appropriate and builds credibility. In the AI Stack section, listing Claude / Dovetail / GPT-4 / Figma AI is correct. For non-technical audiences, the AI Stack section can be skimmed — they will look at the time comparisons and skip the tool names.

**Avoid:**
- Framing AI as "magic" or unexplained capability ("AI-powered insights")
- Implying AI makes design decisions ("The AI suggested..." "AI-generated designs...")
- Over-qualifying ("I use AI but still do real design work") — this is defensive and undermines confidence
- Listing tool names without explaining what each tool does in practical terms

## Approved vs. Not-Approved Examples

These are drawn from actual copy in the current site. Each example shows what was written, a better version, and the specific reason for the change.

---

### Example 1 — Hero Badge

**Section:** Hero (Homepage.tsx, line 423)

**Current copy:**
"AI-Powered UX Design"

**Better version:**
"AI-accelerated design"

**What changed and why:**
"AI-Powered" is a product feature claim — it sounds like a badge from a SaaS vendor. "AI-accelerated" is a process description that puts the human (Harsha) in control and the AI in a supporting role. "Powered by" implies the AI is the engine; "accelerated by" implies the designer is the engine, running faster. The distinction is subtle but consistent with the brand position: Harsha is the senior designer, AI is the workflow advantage.

---

### Example 2 — AI Workflow Section Headline

**Section:** AI Workflow Stack (Homepage.tsx, lines 536–541)

**Current copy:**
"Every phase.
AI-accelerated."

Subhead: "The same rigour as a full design team — at a fraction of the time and cost. AI is embedded in every step, not bolted on at the end."

**Better version:**
Headline stays — it works.

Subhead: "A full-team depth of research, ideation, and documentation — delivered faster because AI handles the parts that shouldn't need a designer's time."

**What changed and why:**
"A fraction of the time and cost" is a price-comparison frame that positions Harsha against cheap services. The brand position is premium quality at better speed — not "cheap." The word "cost" should be avoided unless the audience is specifically comparing hourly rates. The rewrite removes the cost comparison while keeping the speed and quality claim. "Handles the parts that shouldn't need a designer's time" adds the human/AI distinction that builds trust.

---

### Example 3 — Growth Tier Tagline

**Section:** WhoItsFor.tsx (line 39)

**Current copy:**
"Build the design infrastructure that lets your team ship faster."

**Better version:**
"Your design is becoming a bottleneck. Here's how to fix it without hiring."

**What changed and why:**
The current tagline leads with the solution (build infrastructure). The better version leads with the problem the client is already experiencing ("design is becoming a bottleneck"). Per Harsha's own input, clients describe problems, not stages — and growth-stage product leads are acutely aware of this specific bottleneck. The phrase "without hiring" directly addresses the underlying anxiety: they know they need more design capacity, and they're looking for an alternative to headcount. This framing starts the conversation where the client already is.

### Example 4 — Featured Case Study Description

**Section:** Homepage.tsx (line 685) and projects.ts (line 44)

**Current copy (inline on homepage):**
"Unified 3 platforms under one AI-assisted design system — catching accessibility issues before handoff, not after."

**Current copy (projects.ts description):**
"An intelligent design toolchain that uses machine learning to enforce consistency, flag accessibility issues, and generate on-brand component variants — reducing manual review cycles for a fast-moving product team."

**Better version (both):**
Homepage inline: "Three platforms. One coherent system. Zero accessibility regressions at handoff."

projects.ts description: "A growing product team was shipping inconsistent UI across three platforms and catching accessibility failures in QA — the expensive place to find them. The design system audit, rebuild, and AI linting layer changed both."

**What changed and why:**
The current homepage inline is good — concise, outcome-focused. Minor tightening only.

The projects.ts description is tool-forward ("intelligent design toolchain," "machine learning") and describes the solution before establishing the problem. The better version opens with the client's pain (inconsistency, late accessibility failures), names the cost of the problem ("expensive place to find them"), and ends with a teaser that earns the click. It also removes "intelligent design toolchain" — which sounds like marketing copy for a product, not a description of a project Harsha led.

### Example 5 — Contact Section Subhead

**Section:** Homepage.tsx (lines 934–936)

**Current copy:**
"Have a product to design, a system to fix, or a team to support? Tell me what you're working on."

**Better version:**
"Whether you're building from scratch, scaling an existing product, or untangling years of design debt — tell me what's in front of you."

The current copy is good — it asks a question and keeps the reader in their own frame. The better version is slightly stronger because it mirrors the three-tier segmentation in the language clients actually use ("building from scratch" = Startup, "scaling an existing product" = Growth, "untangling design debt" = Growth/Enterprise), without using the tier labels. "Design debt" is language that growth-stage and enterprise clients recognize immediately and respond to — it shows Harsha understands the real problem, not just the abstract challenge. "What's in front of you" is more specific and slightly warmer than "what you're working on."

## Quick Reference

**Always:**
- Lead with the client's problem before introducing the solution
- Pair every metric with a baseline or context
- Name outcomes before naming tools
- Write in first person, specifically, without hedging
- Use "Harsha" — first name only

**Never:**
- Use "passionate," "innovative," "seamless," or "leveraging"
- Show aspirational metrics as verified facts (until real data is confirmed)
- Name AI tools as the subject of a sentence (the designer is the subject; the tool is the instrument)
- Use tier labels (Startup / Growth / Enterprise) in client-facing copy — use problem language instead
- Pad a CTA with "Click here to" or "Learn more about"

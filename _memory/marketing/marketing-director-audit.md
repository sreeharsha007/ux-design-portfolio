## Executive Summary

The portfolio has been built with genuine architectural intelligence — the section sequence, the three-tier segmentation model, the AI workflow positioning, and the contact section's "what happens next" copy are all better than comparable freelance portfolios in this space. However, the site is currently a highly polished shell with a hollow core: every case study is placeholder, every testimonial is fabricated, every headline metric is aspirational, and the nav still displays "Your Name." A professional buyer — the $15k–$50k client this site is architecturally designed to attract — will perform basic due diligence within 60 seconds of arriving and find the inconsistencies. They will close the tab silently. The site will log a bounce from the hero when the real failure is at the trust layer.

The single most important thing Harsha must do before anything else is provide the details of one real project. Not a polished case study — just the raw materials: what the client was trying to do, what Harsha specifically built or decided, and what happened as a result. That one real project unlocks the featured case study, replaces the aspirational hero metric, gives the AI stack section its proof point, and opens the door to requesting a real testimonial from that client. Everything else in this audit — every design fix, every copy improvement, every structural recommendation — is worth less than that one real project.

The site must not be shared with any prospective client in its current state. The structural investment is sound. The content investment is zero. Until that changes, the site actively works against Harsha's goal of moving upmarket.

## Conversion Funnel Map

### Where traffic arrives from

**Primary path:** Upwork profile → "portfolio link" in bio → homepage hero
- Harsha's stated primary acquisition channel is Upwork. The most likely first-touch journey is: a client sees Harsha's Upwork proposal or profile, clicks the portfolio link, arrives on the homepage.
- Current status: **This path is broken.** The site does not link back to Upwork. A client who arrives from Upwork and wants to hire through Upwork has no path back to the Upwork profile. The two channels are completely disconnected.

**Secondary path:** LinkedIn → homepage hero
- LinkedIn is a supporting channel. Clients who are introduced to Harsha professionally or see a post may click through.
- Current status: **Partially broken.** The LinkedIn link in the Contact section goes to https://linkedin.com (the generic homepage), not Harsha's actual profile. A client who clicks it lands on LinkedIn's front page.

**Tertiary path:** Direct search / referral → homepage hero
- A prospective client who has been referred to Harsha or searches for a UX designer may arrive cold.
- Current status: Functional as a landing experience, but the site does not support the due-diligence check (search for Harsha → find consistent professional presence) that this type of client performs.

### Path through the site

Current section order: Hero → AI Workflow Stack → Case Studies → WhoItsFor (Services) → Testimonials → About → Contact

A typical first-visit path for a startup founder:
1. **Hero** — reads "UX Design. AI Speed. Real Results." and the sub-headline naming startups. Sees the floating stat cards. Clicks "See My Work."
2. **Scroll past AI Workflow Stack** — this entire section must be scrolled through to reach the case studies. Many founders drop here. Those who stay see impressive process claims but no attached proof.
3. **Case Studies** — sees four case studies. All are placeholder with stock photography and fabricated metrics. A discerning client recognises template imagery.
4. **WhoItsFor** — if they've reached this point, they self-select a tier and read the deliverables. Strong copy. But there is no pricing signal anywhere to confirm whether they can afford this.
5. **Testimonials** — three testimonials from people who do not exist. A client who googles "Sarah Kim VP of Product Series B SaaS" finds nothing. This section destroys the credibility built by everything above it.
6. **About** — reached by very few visitors (section 7 of 8). No photo, no name, no years of experience stated. Does not mention "Harsha" in the copy at all.
7. **Contact** — primary CTA links to hello@example.com. A client who clicks it and receives a bounce or no reply does not return.

### Where visitors drop off

**Drop-off point 1 — During the AI Workflow Stack section (Section 2)**
The AI Stack is dense, process-heavy, and positioned before the case studies. Startup founders who clicked "See My Work" must scroll through the entire AI Stack before seeing any work. Outcome-focused visitors with low patience leave here.

**Drop-off point 2 — After the Case Studies (Section 3)**
Visitors who wanted to see real work see placeholder projects. Trust fails silently. Bounce.

**Drop-off point 3 — After Testimonials (Section 6)**
The testimonials section is the highest-integrity risk on the site. A visitor who has made it this far — through hero, AI stack, case studies, services — hits three fabricated testimonials. This is where a serious buyer stops being interested and starts being suspicious.

**Drop-off point 4 — Contact section (Section 8)**
A motivated visitor who clicks the primary email CTA reaches hello@example.com. Conversion attempt produces no result. This visitor does not return.

## Cross-Audit Findings — What All Audits Agree On

These are findings that appeared in multiple independent audits. They carry the highest confidence because five specialists identified the same problem from five different angles.

### 1. The site cannot convert without real case studies. (Flagged by: all five audits)

Every specialist audit identified the same root problem: the portfolio has no real work in it. The product strategy audit calls it "a critical failure." The content alignment audit: "the site cannot launch until at least two real case studies are here." The design director: "placeholder case studies are worse than no case studies." The art director: "the most significant piece of work on the site." The UX writer: "launch blocker — do not share the URL." There is no dissent on this point. One real case study, however modest, transforms the site from a template to a portfolio.

### 2. The testimonials section must be hidden before launch. (Flagged by: all five audits)

The fabricated testimonials (Sarah Kim, Marcus Osei, Priya Nair) are confirmed not real. Every audit flagged this. The design director's formulation is the most precise: "fake testimonials are a higher-integrity risk than placeholder case studies — case studies can be anonymised, testimonials with made-up names are straightforwardly dishonest if a client verifies them." The section should be hidden immediately.

### 3. "Your Name" in the navigation must be replaced with "Harsha." (Flagged by: all five audits)

Every audit flagged this as the single quickest trust fix with the highest conversion impact per minute of effort. The design director: "a client who lands on the site and sees 'Your Name' knows instantly this is a template in progress." The UX writer confirms the correct replacement: "Harsha." This takes 30 seconds and is the most embarrassing preventable trust failure on the site.

### 4. The hero stat cards must be replaced or reframed. (Flagged by: product strategy, content alignment, success metrics, design director, UX writer)

The three floating stat cards (40% faster, 20+ projects, 5.0★) are aspirational and unverified. The 5.0★ Upwork claim is directly checkable — a client who searches for Harsha on Upwork and finds no profile or no reviews will not proceed. The success metrics document documents three options in order of preference: replace with real data, reframe as process claims that cannot be falsified, or remove the cards entirely. All audits agree: these cannot go live as-is.

### 5. The primary email CTA is broken. (Flagged by: product strategy, content alignment, design director, UX writer)

hello@example.com is in the primary conversion button and in the project detail page. Every audit flagged it. This is the most mechanical fix on the site — it requires only Harsha providing a real email address.

### 6. The Upwork profile link is completely absent from the site. (Flagged by: product strategy, content alignment, IA, design director, UX writer)

Upwork is Harsha's stated primary acquisition channel. It does not appear anywhere — not in the nav, not in the Contact section, not in the footer. A client who prefers to hire via Upwork has no path from the portfolio to the place where Harsha is most active. Every audit flagged this as a high-severity omission.

### 7. The AI Workflow section has no proof attached to it. (Flagged by: product strategy, content alignment, IA, design director, UX writer)

The AI workflow section is the site's most elaborate and differentiated element. Every audit notes the same gap: it describes tools and phases with no link to a case study that demonstrates the workflow in practice. The section builds interest in a methodology that has no attached evidence. A single bridge — "See how this worked in [Project Name] →" — converts process interest into case study engagement. This change is a quick win once a real case study exists.

### 8. The section order places process before proof. (Flagged by: IA, design director, product strategy, content alignment)

The AI Workflow Stack appears as section 2, before the case studies at section 3. Multiple audits identify this as the primary conversion-order problem: startup founders want to see work first, then understand how it was produced. The IA audit proposes a specific restructured order (Option C: Hero → Case Studies → Testimonials → AI Stack → How I Work → WhoItsFor → About → Pricing → Contact) as the recommended architecture once real case studies exist.

## Positioning Effectiveness

### The claim: "AI-Led UX Designer"

The current positioning is the right direction but lacks the proof that would make it credible and distinctive. Here is an honest assessment of where it lands today versus where it needs to land:

**What is working about the AI positioning:**

The AI workflow section is the most architecturally sophisticated element of any UX freelance portfolio currently in this market segment. The five-phase model with named agents (Research Synthesizer, Insight Clustering, Spec Writer), specific tool pills per phase, and before/after time comparisons is a genuine point of differentiation. No competitor portfolio makes this argument with this level of specificity and visual investment. A technical Product Lead at a Series B company who reads this section would think "this designer thinks in systems, not tasks."

The product strategy audit's assessment is the correct one: "the AI workflow section is a genuine competitive differentiator — if backed by proof."

**What undermines it:**

The AI positioning currently feels like a trend claim because it is unattached to any evidence that the workflow produces results. Every UX freelancer in 2026 mentions AI tools. Harsha's site goes further than most by specifying the tools, the phases, and the time savings — but without a single case study that demonstrates the workflow producing a real client outcome, these remain claims. The gap between the sophistication of the process description and the absence of any proof creates the very impression the site is trying to avoid: a designer who is better at describing what they do than demonstrating it.

**What needs to happen to make the AI positioning credible:**

Three things must happen in sequence:

1. One real case study must exist that explicitly connects the AI workflow to a client outcome. The link does not need to be elaborate — a single sentence in the case study ("Using AI-assisted research synthesis, we compressed a 3-week discovery phase into 4 days, which meant the client had strategy findings before their board meeting instead of after") is more persuasive than the entire current AI Stack section.

2. The AI Stack section needs a bridge CTA to that case study. "See how this worked in [Project Name] →" converts the process interest built by the section into an action.

3. The hero stat cards must reflect this. "Research synthesis in 4 days, not 3 weeks" (a specific, real claim from a real project) is more credible and more distinctive than "40% faster delivery."

Once these three changes are in place, the AI positioning shifts from a trend claim to a demonstrated capability. That transition is the difference between a visitor thinking "interesting, but everyone says that" and thinking "I want to understand how this worked."

## Pricing Signal Strategy

Currently, no pricing information exists anywhere on the site. Budget misalignment is the most common reason a qualified lead does not reach out — they do not want to waste a discovery call if Harsha is outside their budget. A floor price also signals seniority: designers who hide prices are often negotiating downward. Below are three options for communicating value and engagement model without publishing fixed rates.

---

### Option A — Engagement Tier Minimum (Recommended for launch)

**What it looks like on the site:**
A single line added to the CTA area of each WhoItsFor tier:
- Startup: "Fixed-scope projects from $3,000"
- Growth: "Design system and research engagements from $8,000"
- Enterprise: "Monthly retainers and programme work from $15,000 / month"

**What type of visitor it qualifies:**
Founders and product leads who are budget-shopping before reaching out. The floor price is visible at the exact moment they are deciding whether to click the tier CTA. Clients below the floor self-select out. Clients at or above the floor are pre-qualified before the discovery call.

**Conversion trade-off:**
Reduces total inquiry volume. Increases inquiry quality. Eliminates the "I'll reach out and see what the price is" inquiries that waste both parties' time. At the $5k–$50k tier Harsha is targeting, clients expect to see a pricing signal — its absence is conspicuous, not strategic.

---

### Option B — Outcome-Value Framing (Best if Harsha is uncomfortable publishing a floor)

**What it looks like on the site:**
A single section or callout block, positioned between WhoItsFor and Contact:
> "How much does this cost? Every project is different, but most [Startup / Growth / Enterprise] engagements with me are scoped and priced within a week of our first conversation. I'll always give you a fixed price before we start — no hourly billing surprises."

**What type of visitor it qualifies:**
Visitors who want reassurance about process and pricing transparency, not a specific number. This is particularly effective with first-time freelance buyers (common in the startup tier) who are anxious about open-ended costs.

**Conversion trade-off:**
Does not filter budget mismatches before the discovery call. Does reduce friction for visitors who are nervous about the billing model. Lower qualification power than Option A, but higher empathy signal. Best used in combination with a minimum floor rather than as a replacement.

---

### Option C — Comparison Context (Best for positioning against alternatives)

**What it looks like on the site:**
A statement placed near the hero or the WhoItsFor section:
> "A typical design agency quotes 4–6 months and $50k+ for a project like this. I scope the same work in 1–2 weeks and deliver in 6–8 weeks — at a fraction of that cost, because my AI-accelerated workflow removes the overhead."

**What type of visitor it qualifies:**
Startup founders and growth-stage buyers who have already received an agency quote and found it prohibitive. This framing anchors Harsha's price (without naming it) against a known expensive alternative. It is particularly effective for the "I need an investor-ready prototype in 6 weeks and I can't afford an agency" founder.

**Conversion trade-off:**
Strongest persuasion for the primary 90-day audience. Risk: if Harsha's actual prices are not substantially lower than agency prices, this claim will create expectation mismatch in the discovery call. Also positions Harsha explicitly against agencies rather than in a premium independent tier — which may be the right move at the current stage but may need to evolve as Harsha moves upmarket.

---

**Recommendation:** Use Option A (minimum floor) as the baseline for launch, combined with Option B language in the Contact section to address the "how does pricing work?" anxiety. Option C can be added to the hero sub-headline or WhoItsFor section copy once the AI speed positioning is backed by at least one real case study.

## CTA Hierarchy

### Current state

The site has multiple CTAs with inconsistent priority signals and no clear hierarchy. A visitor at any point in the scroll is offered:
- "See My Work" (hero primary)
- "How I Work" (hero secondary)
- "Let's get your MVP designed" / "Let's scale your product design" / "Let's discuss your programme" (WhoItsFor tier CTAs)
- "Start a Conversation" (Contact section)
- "Connect on LinkedIn" (Contact section)
- "Let's Talk" (navigation bar persistent)

Additionally:
- The nav CTA ("Let's Talk") and the contact section CTA ("Start a Conversation") are the same action with different labels — copy inconsistency that makes the site feel undesigned.
- None of the CTAs surfaces Upwork, which is the primary conversion channel.
- There is no single clearly dominant "this is the action" CTA on the page.

### What the hierarchy should be

**Primary conversion action:** Email Harsha directly (the email CTA in the Contact section, linking to a real email address). This is the action Harsha most wants a prospect to take. It should be the most visually prominent CTA on the page, labeled with a specific instruction rather than a social opener.

Recommended label: "Email Harsha directly" or "Start a project conversation" (not "Let's Talk" — which is social — and not "Start a Conversation" — which is passive).

**Secondary conversion action:** Book a 30-minute call (Calendly link, once set up). This is for motivated prospects who want to skip the email exchange and go directly to a conversation. Currently absent from the site. Should be added as a co-equal button next to the primary email CTA in the Contact section.

**Tertiary conversion action:** Hire via Upwork (Upwork profile link). This is for clients who specifically want to transact through Upwork — a meaningful segment of the startup and growth-stage audience. Currently absent from the site entirely. Should be added as a third, smaller CTA in the Contact section, and as a link in the footer replacing the Dribbble placeholder.

**Navigation CTA:** Change "Let's Talk" to "Get in touch" — aligns with the UX writer's recommendation and removes the social opener framing. This persistent CTA should link to the Contact section anchor.

**Section-level CTAs:** The WhoItsFor tier CTAs ("Let's get your MVP designed" etc.) are strong and correctly tier-specific. The UX writer correctly flags the "Let's" prefix as inconsistent with the brand language guide — change to "Get your MVP designed," "Scale your product design," and "Start the conversation."

### Summary

| CTA type | Current state | Recommended state |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | "Start a Conversation" → broken email | "Email Harsha directly" → real email |
| Secondary | "Connect on LinkedIn" → broken URL | "Book a 30-min call" → Calendly (once live) |
| Tertiary | Not present | "Hire me on Upwork" → Upwork profile |
| Nav persistent | "Let's Talk" | "Get in touch" |
| Section-level | "Let's get/scale/discuss" | "Get / Scale / Start" (remove "Let's") |

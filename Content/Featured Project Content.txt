AI-Native Security Intelligence Platform
Finding vulnerabilities was possible. Understanding them in context was not.
I worked on an AI-native security platform designed to help teams identify vulnerabilities across different layers of product development by connecting code, organizational structure, and functional ownership into a more actionable workflow.
At the time, the market had tools that could surface signals, but not many that could connect those signals to ownership, accountability, business impact, and cross-functional action in a way that reflected the context of a specific organization.

Context
The problem was rooted in how modern organizations operate.
Resources were shared across teams and functions. Ownership was often distributed. Costs, accountability, and vulnerabilities were difficult to trace at a granular level. As structures became more layered, it became harder to understand what mattered, who needed to act, and how issues should be prioritized.
Accessing information added another layer of friction. Users often had to move through deep architectural and hierarchical drill-downs just to find the insight they needed.
Root cause analysis was equally fragmented. In practice, RCA is collaborative and cross-functional, but the workflows supporting it did not reflect how teams actually investigate and resolve issues together.

Challenge
The problem was not finding security signals. It was understanding them in context.
Three things made that difficult:
* Risk context was fragmented. Technical findings were visible, but their relationship to ownership, organizational structure, and impact was hard to trace. 
* Accessing insights required too much effort. Critical information was buried inside deep hierarchies, slowing down high-stakes workflows. 
* RCA was collaborative, but the workflow was not. Investigations involved multiple teams, yet the supporting experience was disjointed. 
At the same time, AI introduced a new opportunity to surface intelligence more naturally, but the interaction patterns were still evolving. That made trust, clarity, and feedback essential.

What I Led
I contributed across product definition, workflow design, delivery readiness, and system scalability.
My work included:
* Defining the MVP through client workshops, alignment sessions, and prioritization exercises that helped shape a funding-ready scope 
* Shaping the product direction through market analysis, AI trend mapping, and security landscape research 
* Defining key journeys through workflow mapping, journey breakdowns, and collaborative RCA flow design 
* Improving UI delivery readiness through implementation-aware handoff, structured specifications, and reusable interaction patterns 
* Building a scalable design foundation through design system thinking, reusable components, and foundational patterns for future modules 

Key Decisions
Making organizational complexity more intelligible
I introduced a semantic layer during onboarding and mapping to better structure organizational information for both users and AI. This created a stronger basis for linking vulnerabilities to ownership, accountability, cost visibility, and business context.
Reducing dependence on drill-down navigation
To reduce the effort needed to retrieve insights, the experience introduced both global and contextual AI chat. This gave users a faster way to access answers and updates without removing the ability to drill deeper when needed.
Reframing RCA around collaboration
A key gap emerged in how root cause analysis was being performed. Since RCA is typically cross-functional, I helped shape an AI-powered canvas that better supported investigation in context, team collaboration, and shareable outputs for quicker resolution.
Grounding AI interactions in usability principles
Because AI UI patterns were still maturing, I used HCI principles to shape interactions that felt more understandable, responsive, and trustworthy in a high-stakes environment.

What Changed
The work helped turn an ambitious concept into a more structured and scalable MVP direction.
* Organizational context became more usable. The semantic layer gave the system a better understanding of how teams, resources, ownership, and vulnerabilities related to one another. 
* Insight access became faster. Global and contextual AI chat reduced the need to manually navigate deep hierarchies just to retrieve information. 
* RCA became more aligned with real workflows. The canvas supported a more collaborative investigation model and made it easier to turn analysis into shareable outputs. 
* Delivery became more scalable. A stronger design system and cleaner UI handoff reduced friction between design and implementation. 
* The MVP became clearer. Product framing, workflow definition, and prioritization helped shape a more credible direction for funding and future growth. 

Designing for AI
One of the most important parts of this project was designing in a space where AI interaction patterns were still taking shape.
Rather than treating AI as a layer on top of the interface, I approached it as part of the workflow itself. That required careful thinking around trust, system feedback, usability, and user control.
This project reinforced that in AI-led experiences, capability alone is not enough. Users also need clarity, orientation, and confidence in how the system is helping them.

Next
The next phase focused on deepening the investigation workflow.
Planned enhancements included:
* richer canvas capabilities 
* stronger RCA-to-deployment flows 
* continued refinement of AI-led interaction patterns as the product matured 

Reflection
This project reinforced how important it is to design AI experiences around clarity, feedback, and control, especially in complex and high-stakes environments.
It also highlighted a broader learning: when designing emerging AI products, the challenge is not only shaping the intelligence itself, but shaping the interaction patterns that help people trust and use that intelligence effectively.
